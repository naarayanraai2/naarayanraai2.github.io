About Me

I am a Ph.D. student and Graduate Research Assistant in the Department of Civil & Environmental Engineering at the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee. My research sits at the intersection of Mechanical Engineering (my undergraduate background) and Smart Transportation Systems. Currently, I focus on the experimental validation of multi-vehicle coordination and the impact of Connected and Autonomous Vehicles (CAVs) on traffic flow dynamics.

At UWM, I led the development of a custom-scaled RC car experimental platform to bridge the gap between theoretical control algorithms and real-world traffic phenomena. By implementing game-theoretic frameworks like AdaptiveSeek using ROS, Python, and C++, I work to mitigate classical traffic issues such as stop-and-go shockwaves. My goal is to design resilient, cooperative autonomous systems that enhance the stability and efficiency of modern urban infrastructure.

ACEMS Lab , Directed by Xiaowei (Tom) Shi, is actively seeking students. If you are interested in joining UWM and working in the research group, please feel free to contact and send your CV via email: tomshi@uwm.edu.

My research aims to advance transportation safety and system understanding by integrating experimental vehicle platforms with large-scale telematics data. By bridging controlled physical testing and real-world driving behavior, I seek to develop data-driven insights that support safer, more efficient, and more resilient transportation systems.

My research framework combines experimental validation, data analytics, and transportation systems modeling. I leverage scaled vehicle testbeds to study vehicle dynamics and traffic interactions under controlled conditions, while simultaneously analyzing large telematics datasets to capture real-world driving behavior at scale. This dual approach enables robust model validation, deeper insight into driver behavior, and the translation of empirical findings into practical transportation safety applications.

Experimental Vehicle Testbeds

This research direction focuses on the design and use of scaled autonomous and semi-autonomous vehicle platforms to investigate vehicle dynamics, control strategies, and traffic interactions. Using 1/10-scale RC vehicles equipped with sensors and onboard computation, I conduct controlled experiments to replicate real-world traffic scenarios. These testbeds enable systematic evaluation of control algorithms, traffic behavior models, and safety-related vehicle responses in a cost-effective and repeatable environment.

Telematics-Based Traffic Safety Analytics

This direction centers on the analysis of large-scale vehicle telematics data to understand driving behavior and its relationship to traffic safety outcomes. By examining acceleration patterns, temporal driving dynamics, and contextual roadway information, I identify behavioral indicators associated with elevated safety risk. The insights derived from this work support data-driven transportation planning, infrastructure design, and policy development aimed at reducing crashes and improving roadway performance.
